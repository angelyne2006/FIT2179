When Rain Becomes a Disaster

Understanding Malaysia's Flood Crisis

Malaysia floods every year — but how bad is it,
and who bears the cost?

Intro

Introduction

Every year, Malaysia's Northeast Monsoon brings torrential rain to the East Coast — and with it, floods that displace thousands, destroy homes, and cost the nation hundreds of millions of ringgit. This visualisation traces the 2024–2025 flood season: where the rain fell hardest, who was displaced, and what it cost.

01

Malaysia's Monsoon: When and Where Rain Becomes a Threat

Malaysia's floods are driven by the Northeast Monsoon.
It brings heavy rainfall to East Coast states between November and March.

Every state exceeded its historical rainfall average in 2025,
with Terengganu and Pahang recording the highest anomalies.

Rainfall peaks sharply in November during the Northeast Monsoon,
with average rainfall nearly doubling compared to mid-year months.

Kelantan and Terengganu consistently record the highest rainfall
across all months, driven by their East Coast exposure.

02

When Floods Hit: The Human and Environmental Impact

When the rains come, consequences extend beyond water levels —
homes are destroyed, people displaced, and the land itself suffers.

Terengganu and Kelantan recorded the highest flood losses in 2025,
reflecting their direct exposure to the Northeast Monsoon.

Urban states like W.P. KL and Pulau Pinang show the lowest vegetation
health, while Sabah and Sarawak remain the greenest regions.

Sabah experienced six separate evacuation events between January and May 2025,
the most of any state. Note: A further 143,927 evacuees were recorded across
multiple states in December 2024 at the Northeast Monsoon peak.

While total flood losses fell from 2024 to 2025, infrastructure losses
rose — suggesting floods cause deeper structural damage over time.

03

The Price Malaysia Pays: Economic Damage

Floods cost Malaysia hundreds of millions of ringgit every year —
but the burden is not shared equally across states.

2021 saw catastrophic losses of RM6.1 billion from Tropical Depression 29W.
Losses have since stabilised at RM600–900 million annually.

Perlis bears the highest per capita burden at RM100.74 per person,
far exceeding larger states despite its lower total losses.

Terengganu and Kelantan top the losses ranking, collectively accounting
for over 20% of total flood losses across all states in 2025.
